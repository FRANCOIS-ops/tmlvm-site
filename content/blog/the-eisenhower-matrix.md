Eisenhower.

The name rings a bell.

He led Allied forces in Europe during WWII, then became President of the United States.

He had this famous sentence:
"I have two kinds of problems: the urgent and the important. The urgent are not important, and the important are never urgent."

When you have a war to win, you'd better tackle the important problems.
Yet you often end up stuck dealing with urgent tasks that don’t matter much.

How do you exit the trap?
Stephen Covey, in *The 7 Habits of Highly Effective People*, developed a decision matrix based on this idea.

It’s very simple:
one axis for importance, one for urgency.
It gives you four boxes.

But its simplicity hides its real power, if you actually use it.
It helps you avoid being ripped apart by small, urgent tasks that shout at you.
And it forces you to invest time proactively in things that are not yet urgent, but matter a lot.
Like changing the oil in your car: not urgent… until it’s too late.

It’s also a great tool to discuss with your manager and align on priorities and emergencies.

Here are four questions to help you put tasks in the right box:
- “What do I absolutely need to do today?” → Urgent and important. Start with it. 
- “What is important that I would do if I had more time?” → Not urgent but important. If you don’t plan it, you’ll never do it, and regret it later. 
- “If I had a heart attack and could work only two hours a day, would I still do this task?” → If the answer is no, then it’s not important, even if it’s urgent. 
- “If I’m honest, am I doing this just because it’s fun?” → That’s why we do non-urgent, non-important tasks.
